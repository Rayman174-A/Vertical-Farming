Tulsi: India’s herbal Deity

The ancient texts of Vedas and Puranas state that the number of gods and goddesses in the Hindu pantheon is about 330 million. A sizeable collection combined with the mythological aspect is bound to broaden one's perspective with regards to the existence of deities in various forms. A divinity being worshipped in various forms is not simply a localized tradition in Hindu culture, similar practices are observed in Japan with respect to Shinto believes about Kami, spirits residing in plants, animals or effigies. One such example is Tulsi, a plant local to Southeast Asia and worshipped all across India as a deity. Tulsi’s mythological lore involves it being the bride of Lord Vishnu when he is in the Shaligram form. This might be fitting mythological tale to show reverence to this valuable plant but it does not shed any light on the cultural or scientific properties and influences this herb possesses. Tulsi is a culturally profound plant, known all across the world as Holy Basil. Tulsi’s scientific nomenclate is Ocimum tenuiflorum or its synonym Ocimum sanctum and it belongs to the Lamiaceae family of flora. Astonishingly Tulsi is a close relative to mint, despite the difference in the profiles of both herbs. While Tulsi can also be classified as a subshrub due to its anatomical morphology and phyllotaxy, in simpler words Tulsi is a multi-branched plant with green or purple oval-shaped leaves which are slightly toothed at the margin. Tulsi also possesses a strong, distinct and characteristic smell. There are broadly three varieties of Tulsi, namely Rama Tulsi, Krishna Tulsi and the Vana Tulsi. Rama tulsi’s characteristics are broad green leaves with slightly sweet taste while Krishna Tulsi can be identified by the purplish green leaves and Vana Tulsi is the one that grows in the wild. Tulsi is one of the very few deities who have a major role in the diet, pun totally intended. Tulsi is involved in medicine, culinary arts and cultural ceremonies for ages. In culinary and cuisine sphere, Tulsi is a versatile ingredient added in tea and also in Thai cookery alike. From a medical standpoint, Tulsi has been an age-old part of Ayurveda and homoeopathy, it has also established its dominance in household remedies especially in pulmonary issues. Tulsi has been a quintessential part of Indian household for ages owning a to it's versatility as a herb. It finds applications as a bug repellent, a source of essential oils and a distinct fragrance that is slightly sweet. Along with all these properties it is a very convenient plant to grow in closures as well. Apart from the cultural aspect, due to its merits, Tulsi has earned its godhood in every sense.
